\title{Antisocial but inconsequential: large language model agents punish cooperators yet neither sustain cooperation nor reproduce its cross-cultural geography}

\author{Author 1$^{1}$}

\address{$^{1}$Affiliation, City, Country}

\subject{behavioural ecology, computational social science, artificial intelligence}

\keywords{large language models, public-goods game, antisocial punishment, cooperation, multi-agent simulation, cross-cultural variation}

\corres{Corresponding author\\
\email{tcnguyen2365@gmail.com}}

\begin{abstract}
Costly peer punishment is a cornerstone human institution for sustaining cooperation, yet its effectiveness and its antisocial misuse vary sharply across societies. We ask whether large language model (LLM) agents reproduce this institution and its cultural structure by running three open instruction-tuned models (Qwen2.5-7B, Gemma-2-9B, Llama-3.1-8B) through Herrmann's public-goods game with peer punishment, crossed with five languages, four dispositions and sixteen neutral society personas drawn from the original participant cities. The agents contribute generously (baseline means 14.26, 13.41 and 10.67 of 20 tokens against a human grand-mean of 8.6) and they punish heavily, but a majority of all deduction points (54--59\%) are antisocial, aimed at members who contributed at least as much as the punisher, rivalling the most punitive human pools. Two diagnostic human results nonetheless fail to emerge. Punishment does not raise cooperation (P$-$N $=-0.36$, $-3.55$ and $+0.08$; Gemma's drop $p<0.001$; equivalence tests bound the other two effects below half the human gain) against a human gain of $+4.3$, and the cross-societal signature collapses: the human antisocial-punishment--cooperation coupling ($\rho\approx-0.90$) vanishes ($\rho=-0.09$, $-0.15$, $+0.30$) and the geography of cooperation disappears. LLM agents reproduce the existence of (anti)social punishment but neither its institutional function nor its cultural structure.
\end{abstract}

\begin{fmtext}
Among the most striking findings in the experimental study of human cooperation is that people will pay to punish free-riders, that this costly punishment can stabilise contributions to a public good, and that its character varies markedly across societies---in some participant pools punishment is turned not against shirkers but against generous cooperators, an antisocial impulse that tracks weak norms of civic cooperation. As large language model agents are increasingly proposed as proxies for human decision-makers, it is natural to ask whether they reproduce this institution: do they punish at all, do they punish the right targets, does punishment sustain their cooperation as it does ours, and does its cultural geography survive when the same model speaks as a participant from sixteen different cities? Here we put three open models through Herrmann's classic design to find out.
\end{fmtext}

\section{Introduction}

The provision of public goods poses one of the most enduring problems in the social and biological sciences: rational individuals who share in a collective benefit have a private incentive to withhold their own contribution, so that universal free-riding can unravel an outcome from which all would gain \cite{ledyard1995public, dawes1980social}. In the laboratory, this tension is distilled in the linear public-goods game, where players repeatedly decide how much of a private endowment to invest in a common project whose returns are shared regardless of investment; absent further structure, contributions decay towards the free-riding equilibrium over successive rounds \cite{ledyard1995public}. A large body of work shows that this decay can be arrested when players are permitted to punish one another at a personal cost, sacrificing their own payoff to reduce that of low contributors: such costly peer punishment functions as a decentralised institution that sustains cooperation where material self-interest alone would erode it \cite{fehr2000cooperation, fehr2002altruistic}, and a substantial evolutionary literature has since examined when costly punishment and related institutional designs can emerge and stabilise collective action \cite{han2015avoiding,han2016synergy,song2026punishment}. Yet the form this institution takes is far from universal. In a sixteen-society comparison spanning participant pools from Boston to Athens to Riyadh, \cite{herrmann2008antisocial} documented striking cross-societal heterogeneity, including widespread \emph{antisocial} punishment---deduction points aimed at cooperators who contributed as much as or more than the punisher---whose prevalence covaried negatively with the cooperation a society could ultimately achieve. The capacity of a punishment institution to support collective action thus depends not merely on its existence but on culturally structured norms governing who gets punished and why, in line with broader accounts of how communities sustain commons through locally tailored rules and sanctioning \cite{ostrom1990governing}.

Recent advances in large language models (LLMs) have prompted a growing interest in using them as simulated subjects for the social and behavioural sciences. Because an instruction-tuned model can be conditioned on a persona and asked to respond to the same materials presented to human participants, researchers have proposed treating LLM outputs as ``silicon samples'' that may reproduce population-level patterns of attitude and behaviour \cite{argyle2023out}. Such samples have been used to emulate survey respondents and economic agents \cite{horton2023llm}, to populate generative agent societies whose interactions exhibit emergent social dynamics \cite{park2023generative}, and to replicate classic findings from psychology and experimental economics with surprising fidelity \cite{aher2023using}. At the same time, the behavioural validity of these simulations remains contested: models can match aggregate human tendencies on some tasks while diverging systematically on others, silicon-sample conclusions can hinge on defensible-but-arbitrary analytic choices \cite{cummins2025threat}, and the conditions under which silicon samples track---rather than merely caricature---human behaviour are still being mapped \cite{mei2024turing,hullman2026validating}. Establishing where the correspondence holds and where it breaks is therefore a precondition for any scientific use of LLM agents as stand-ins for human populations, and a central question for the emerging social physics of artificial intelligence \cite{han2026socialphysics}.

A natural testing ground is the multi-agent strategic game, and a number of studies have begun to place LLMs in repeated and one-shot interactions to probe their cooperative and competitive tendencies \cite{akata2023playing, brookins2023playing, feng2024survey}. This work has shown that models can be induced to cooperate, defect, and respond to opponents' moves, and that their choices are sensitive to prompt framing, to the language of play and to the social roles they are assigned \cite{leng2023llm, tjuatja2024llms, buscemi2025communication}; game-theoretic audits have likewise begun to map systematic cooperation biases and incentive sensitivity across models and languages \cite{huynh2025understanding, huynh2026payoff}. Sanctioning itself has recently begun to attract attention: reasoning-class models free-ride when allowed to choose between sanctioning and sanction-free institutions \cite{piedrahita2025corrupted}, punishment strategies shape the emergence of cooperative norms in LLM-agent societies and common-pool settings \cite{warnakulasuriya2025evolution, gupta2025norm}, generative agent-based models can replicate landmark third-party-punishment experiments under persona prompting \cite{cross2025validating}, and LLMs punish unfairness more severely than humans in third-party settings \cite{liu2025outraged}. What remains untested, however, is whether LLM agents reproduce the deeper \emph{institutional function} of a sanctioning mechanism rather than its surface behaviour---whether granting them the means to punish actually sustains cooperation as it does for humans---and whether they reproduce the culturally heterogeneous punishment norms, including the antisocial/prosocial decomposition of punishment, that distinguish one human society from another. These are precisely the diagnostic features that make peer punishment a substantive scientific phenomenon rather than a generic propensity to penalise, and they have gone largely untested across models, languages, and simulated societies---even though survey evidence already suggests that country personas only partially shift the culturally homogeneous default profiles of current models \cite{tao2024cultural}.

We address this gap with a faithful replication of \cite{herrmann2008antisocial} on the FAIRGAME multi-agent framework \cite{fairgame2025}. Groups of four agents play a ten-period public-goods game under partner matching, each period receiving a fresh endowment of twenty tokens to allocate between a private account and a group project with a marginal per-capita return of $0.4$. We run two treatments: a baseline N treatment with contribution only, and a P treatment that adds a costly punishment stage in which each agent may assign zero to ten deduction points to each other member at a one-to-three cost ratio, with co-players relabelled every period to block identity tracking. The full design crosses these treatments with three open instruction-tuned models---Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct, Gemma-2-9B-it, and Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct \cite{qwen2025, gemma2024, llama2024}---five languages, four behavioural dispositions, and sixteen neutral society personas drawn from Herrmann's participant cities, each stated as a bare geographic origin with no behavioural stereotype, for ten groups per condition.

Our central contribution is a clear dissociation between the existence of (anti)social punishment and its institutional function and cultural structure. The agents do contribute and do punish, and a majority of their deduction points---fifty-four to fifty-nine per cent across models---is antisocial, directed at co-players who contributed at least as much as the punisher, a share that rivals the most punitive human pools. But the two diagnostic human results fail to emerge. First, the punishment institution does not raise cooperation: the P-minus-N change in mean contribution is $-0.36$ for Qwen (not significant), $-3.55$ for Gemma (Mann--Whitney $p<0.001$), and $+0.08$ for Llama (not significant), against a human grand-mean gain of $+4.3$, even though baseline contributions are high (N-treatment means of $14.26$, $13.41$, and $10.67$ of twenty, versus a human grand mean of $8.6$); the contribution-by-period and deviation-binned punishment patterns are summarised in figure~\ref{fig:punishment} and table~\ref{tab:baseline}. Second, the cross-societal signature collapses: the strong human negative coupling between antisocial punishment and cooperation ($\rho \approx -0.90$) is absent in the models ($\rho = -0.09$, $-0.15$, and $+0.30$), the ``geography of cooperation'' disappears as sixteen city personas shift mean contribution by only about one token, and LLM-versus-human cross-societal rank correlations are merely $+0.33$, $+0.06$, and $+0.17$ (figure~\ref{fig:cross}). LLM agents thus reproduce the \emph{existence} of (anti)social punishment but neither its institutional \emph{function} of sustaining cooperation nor its cultural \emph{structure}, which we take as the explicit validity criterion against which silicon-sample replications of human cooperation experiments should be judged.

\section{Discussion}

The public-goods game with peer punishment offers one of the most demanding behavioural benchmarks in experimental economics, because it couples a first-order cooperation decision with a second-order sanctioning decision and asks whether the latter can stabilise the former. Our results show that large language model agents clear the surface of this benchmark while failing its diagnostic interior. The agents contribute, and contribute generously: baseline mean contributions in the contribution-only treatment reach 14.26, 13.41 and 10.67 tokens of the 20-token endowment for Qwen, Gemma and Llama respectively, all comfortably above the human grand mean of 8.6. The agents also punish, and a clear majority of the deduction points they spend are directed at group members who contributed an amount equal to or greater than the punisher's own contribution. This antisocial share lies between 54\% and 59\% across the three models, placing every agent population at or beyond the antisocial-punishment extreme of the human cross-cultural distribution, rivalling the most punitive participant pools in \textcite{herrmann2008antisocial}. In the narrow sense of behavioural repertoire, then, the agents reproduce the existence of both prosocial and antisocial punishment.

What they do not reproduce are the two results that make the human data theoretically load-bearing. The first is institutional. In human groups, the option to punish raises cooperation: across the sixteen societies of \textcite{herrmann2008antisocial}, opening the sanctioning stage lifts mean contributions by roughly 4.3 tokens, and in the canonical partner-matching design of \textcite{fehr2000cooperation} punishment converts a decaying public good into a stable one. Our agents show no such institutional function. The contrast between the punishment and no-punishment treatments, P~$-$~N, is $-$0.36 for Qwen and $+$0.08 for Llama, neither significantly different from zero, and $-$3.55 for Gemma, a significant \emph{decrease} (Mann--Whitney $p<0.001$; table~\ref{tab:baseline}, figure~\ref{fig:punishment}). The sanctioning institution that defines the human result is, for these agents, either inert or actively corrosive. Because so much of the punishment they deliver lands on equal and high contributors rather than on free-riders, the threat that should discipline defection instead penalises cooperation, and a cooperator weighing whether to give has no reason to expect that giving will be rewarded or that withholding will be punished. The behaviour is present; the conditional-cooperation logic that would let it do institutional work is absent.

The second failure is cultural. The most distinctive finding of \textcite{herrmann2008antisocial} is not that antisocial punishment exists but that its prevalence varies systematically across societies and is tightly, negatively coupled to cooperation: where citizens punish high contributors, the public good collapses, yielding a cross-societal Spearman correlation of roughly $-$0.90 between antisocial punishment and contribution, and a recognisable ``geography of cooperation'' in which civic norms and rule of law track behaviour. None of this structure survives the transfer to silicon. Across our sixteen neutral city personas, the agent-level coupling between antisocial punishment and cooperation is effectively nil ($\rho = -0.09$, $-0.15$ and $+0.30$ for Qwen, Gemma and Llama; figure~\ref{fig:cross}), and the geography itself flattens: changing the city named in the persona moves mean contribution by only about one token. This is consistent with survey-based evidence that cultural prompting only partially aligns LLM outputs with country-level human value profiles \cite{tao2024cultural}, although synthetic cultural agents have shown qualitative resemblance to non-WEIRD behaviour in simpler two-player fairness games \cite{gonzalezbonorino2025nonweird}. When we rank the sixteen societies by agent cooperation and correlate those ranks against the human ordering, the agreement is negligible ($\rho = +0.33$, $+0.06$ and $+0.17$), and the absolute levels differ significantly for two of three models (Wilcoxon $p=0.005$ for Gemma, $p=0.025$ for Llama). The agents do not merely compress the human cross-cultural variance; they fail to reconstruct its ordering at all. The internal structure of punishment tells the same story. Only Gemma reproduces the human monotone gradient in which deeper free-riding is punished harder (3.71 points for the largest negative deviations versus 2.45 for moderate ones, while sparing equal contributors at 1.40 points); Qwen and Llama punish almost flatly across deviation bins (roughly 2.15--2.78 and 2.53--2.86 points), hitting cooperators nearly as hard as defectors. The deviation-sensitivity that gives human punishment its corrective meaning is, for two of three models, simply not there.

Taken together, these findings draw a sharp line between reproducing a behaviour and reproducing its function. It is increasingly common to treat LLM agents as ``silicon subjects'' whose responses can stand in for human participants in survey and economic experiments \parencite{argyle2023out,horton2023llm,aher2023using,park2023generative}. Our results sound a specific caution for that programme. The agents pass the descriptive test---they produce contribution and punishment distributions that, viewed marginally, look broadly human and even capture the surprising prevalence of antisocial sanctioning. Yet the two relational properties that any explanatory use of the paradigm depends upon---that punishment causally sustains cooperation, and that its antisocial form varies across cultures and predicts cooperative collapse---are exactly the properties that vanish. A silicon-subject study that read off only the marginals would conclude, wrongly, that these agents recover the Herrmann result; the failure is visible only in the conditional and cross-societal structure. Behavioural realism at the level of distributions is therefore a weak guarantee of mechanistic realism, and the use of LLM agents to forecast institutional outcomes should be validated against precisely the diagnostic, relational signatures that distinguish competing theories---in line with recent statistical frameworks for validating LLM simulations as behavioural evidence \parencite{hullman2026validating} and with warnings that silicon-sample results can hinge on analytic flexibility \parencite{cummins2025threat}. A companion study reaches the same conclusion for catastrophe-risk sensitivity in the collective-risk climate dilemma, where the same models reproduce high cooperation but not its dependence on risk \parencite{companionCRSD2026}.

The implications extend beyond the methodology of computational social science to the engineering of multi-agent LLM systems. There is growing interest in populations of language agents that negotiate, divide resources and self-govern \parencite{park2023generative,vezhnevets2023generative,akata2023playing,leng2023llm,hammond2025multiagent,piatti2024cooperate,gupta2025norm}, and costly peer punishment is one of the few decentralised institutions known to stabilise cooperation among self-interested humans without a central authority. Our results indicate that this institution does not transfer for free. When the same sanctioning channel that is supposed to discipline defectors is instead used, the majority of the time, against good citizens---and when, for one model, equipping agents with punishment lowers the collective payoff relative to no punishment at all---the mechanism that designers might reach for to keep an agent society cooperative becomes a liability rather than a safeguard. A multi-agent system that grants its members the ability to sanction one another cannot assume that the ability will be exercised correctively; absent the conditional-cooperation and deviation-tracking logic that humans bring to the task, sanctioning power may amplify rather than damp conflict, turning the punishment institution into a tax on cooperation---a concrete instance of the broader lesson that mechanisms promoting cooperation need not promote social welfare \cite{han2024welfare}.

\subsection{Limitations}

Several limitations bound the scope of these conclusions. First, we study three open-weight instruction-tuned models in the 7--9B parameter range (Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct, Gemma-2-9B-it, Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct; \cite{qwen2025,gemma2024,llama2024}) at a single decoding temperature of 0.8. We cannot say how much of the diagnostic failure reflects model scale or sampling, and it remains possible that larger frontier systems would recover more of the institutional and cultural structure; our claims are therefore claims about these models under these settings, not about language agents in general. Second, our societal manipulation is deliberately minimal: each persona names only a city and country from Herrmann's participant pool, with no behavioural stereotype attached. This neutrality is a design choice that lets us ask whether a bare geographic label, of the kind a silicon-subject study might casually deploy, suffices to elicit cultural variation---and our null result speaks directly to that question. But it also means we have not tested whether richer, norm-laden personas could recover the geography of cooperation, and the absence of cross-societal heterogeneity should be read as a statement about neutral labels rather than about the models' ceiling for cultural differentiation. Third, format non-compliance is negligible in the English baseline that carries the headline results (at most 1.8\% of contribution decisions and 6.8\% of punishment decisions per model) but rises sharply in some low-resource pairings, reaching 31\% of contribution and 43\% of punishment decisions for Gemma in Arabic and 15--19\% of contribution decisions for Llama in Vietnamese; although we log every cell's rate as a joint data-quality and fairness signal, the affected cross-language cells carry correspondingly fewer clean decisions, and non-compliance may interact unevenly with personas and dispositions. Fourth, although the same four agents stay together for all ten periods, the design---faithful to the original---blocks communication, explicit agreements and cross-period identity tracking (co-players are re-labelled every period), so the rich reciprocity and reputational dynamics that scaffold human punishment are unavailable by construction. Fifth, like all prompted-LLM studies our results carry some prompt sensitivity, and the natural-language framing of the brief, history and disposition block could shift behaviour in ways we have not exhaustively mapped. Sixth, the original experiment is within-subject---each participant played both the no-punishment and punishment treatments as two counterbalanced ten-period sequences---so the human P~$-$~N gain is a within-subject contrast that also absorbs sequence-order and experience effects, whereas our agents play the two treatments as independent groups; the institutional effect we report is therefore a between-group difference and the human benchmark it is compared against is not a perfectly like-for-like estimand, although the qualitative conclusion (the punishment institution does not lift cooperation for these agents) does not depend on the distinction. Finally, instruction-tuned models are shaped by alignment training that explicitly discourages harmful or punitive outputs and encourages even-handedness, which may simultaneously inflate baseline contributions, blunt the deviation-sensitivity of punishment, and homogenise behaviour across personas; disentangling alignment effects from genuine strategic reasoning is beyond what our design can resolve.

These limitations chart the natural next steps. Personas richer than a neutral city label---carrying explicit social norms, institutional context or cultural values---would test whether the geography of cooperation is genuinely unreachable or merely under-elicited by bare geographic framing. Allowing communication, explicit agreements and cross-period reciprocity would let agents build the reputational scaffolding on which human punishment depends, and would reveal whether the institutional function of sanctioning emerges once that scaffolding is in place. Agents whose policies are shaped by reinforcement learning in the game itself, rather than by prompting alone, would separate what current models do by default from what language agents can be trained to do. And extending the battery to frontier models would establish whether the diagnostic failures we report shrink with scale or persist as a structural feature of LLM agents.

\section{Conclusion}

In Herrmann's public-goods game with costly peer punishment, large language model agents reproduce the existence of (anti)social punishment but neither its institutional function nor its cultural structure. They contribute generously and they punish, and a majority of their punishment---54\% to 59\% of all deduction points---is antisocial, aimed at members who gave as much as or more than the punisher, rivalling the most punitive human pools. Yet the punishment institution does not raise cooperation, and for one model significantly lowers it; the strong cross-societal coupling between antisocial punishment and cooperation collapses from a human $\rho\approx-0.90$ to near zero; and the geography of cooperation flattens, with sixteen neutral personas moving contribution by about a single token. The agents reproduce the behaviour while losing the relational logic that makes it matter. For silicon-subject research this means that human-looking marginals are no guarantee of human-like mechanism, and that the diagnostic, conditional structure of a paradigm is where validation must focus. For multi-agent LLM systems it means that the sanctioning institutions humans use to stabilise cooperation cannot be assumed to transfer---granted the power to punish, today's agents are as likely to turn it on good citizens as on free-riders.
